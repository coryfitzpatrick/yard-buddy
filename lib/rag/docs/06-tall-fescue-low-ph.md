# Tall Fescue Low-pH Management (NCSU TurfFiles + PSU Extension)

Tall fescue is a cool-season grass widely grown in NC Piedmont and Mountain regions (USDA Zones 6–8a) and across the cool-season transition zone. Optimal soil pH for tall fescue is **6.0–6.5 (NCSU)** / **6.0–7.0 acceptable, 6.5 optimum (Penn State)**. NC clay soils typically run acidic and benefit from periodic lime applications.

## Soil testing — REQUIRED FIRST STEP for pH correction

The most important step in any pH-correction program is a current soil test that reports **buffer pH**, not just soil pH. **Without buffer pH data, a specific lime rate cannot be calculated accurately.**

- **North Carolina:** NCDA&CS Agronomic Division provides FREE soil testing April 1 – November 30 (small fee Dec–March). Submit via local county Cooperative Extension office or directly to the Raleigh lab. Standard turnaround: **2–4 weeks** during peak season. NCDA&CS reports both pH AND buffer pH (Mehlich buffer), which is what's required for accurate lime rate calculation.
- **Pennsylvania / general:** Penn State Agricultural Analytical Services Lab uses the Adams–Evans buffer test. ~$10/sample, 2-week turnaround.
- **Other states:** request "buffer pH" or "lime requirement" explicitly on the submission form — basic pH-only tests do NOT include buffer pH and cannot be used for precise lime rates.

## Lime rate methodology — BUFFER pH determines the rate

**Per NCSU and Penn State extension methodology, lime rate is a FUNCTION of buffer pH AND target pH AND soil texture, not soil pH alone.** Two soils at the same measured pH can require dramatically different lime amounts depending on cation exchange capacity (CEC) and buffering capacity — high-clay, high-organic-matter soils need much more lime to shift one pH unit than sandy soils do.

### Two paths for the homeowner

**Path A — RECOMMENDED: wait for buffer pH before any lime application.**
- Order soil test with buffer pH (NCDA&CS for NC, PSU AASL for PA, your state's Cooperative Extension lab elsewhere).
- When results arrive, use the lab's lime requirement value directly (it will be reported as lbs/1,000 sq ft or tons/acre).
- This is the agronomically correct path. The 2–4 week delay is short relative to the 3–6 month timeframe for pH change.

**Path B — Conservative blind application when soil test must be deferred.**
- Apply a **CONSERVATIVE** initial dose: **25–30 lbs calcitic lime per 1,000 sq ft**, NOT 40–50 lbs.
- 25–30 lbs is the "safe under-application" range that will not over-shoot pH on lightly-buffered (sandy) soils. Heavy clay soils may need more, but the second application after retesting can deliver that.
- DO NOT recommend 40–50 lbs per 1,000 sq ft without buffer pH data — even though this stays below the 50 lb safety cap, it risks over-liming sandy or low-CEC soils, which is **harder to reverse** than under-liming.
- Retest with buffer pH at 6 months; apply additional lime per lab calculation if pH is still below 6.0.
- NEVER exceed 50 lbs lime per 1,000 sq ft in any single application (NCSU + PSU upper safety limit, regardless of buffer pH).
- If the lab eventually reports a calculated rate above 50 lbs/1,000 sq ft, SPLIT into two applications 4–6 months apart.

## Calcitic vs dolomitic lime — calcitic is the DEFAULT

- **Default recommendation: CALCITIC lime** (calcium carbonate, CaCO3)
- Dolomitic lime (calcium magnesium carbonate, CaMg(CO3)2) is appropriate ONLY when a soil test confirms Mg deficiency
- Applying dolomitic when Mg is adequate causes excess Mg, which inhibits Ca and K uptake
- Do NOT recommend dolomitic based on regional assumptions alone ("Southeast soils often have low Mg"). Soil test required to confirm Mg status.

**Product accuracy:**
- **Pennington Fast Acting Lime is CALCITIC** (pelletized calcium carbonate with carrier). Do NOT recommend it as a dolomitic lime source. The "fast acting" marketing language refers to the carrier dispersion mechanism, not a faster pH shift — pH change still takes 3–6 months in typical clay soils.
- **Greenview Mag-I-Cal contains MAGNESIUM** (calcium-magnesium product). Do NOT classify Mag-I-Cal as calcitic — its magnesium content makes it functionally dolomitic-style. Only recommend Mag-I-Cal when a soil test confirms Mg deficiency; otherwise prefer a true calcitic source.
- Dolomitic lime brand examples: Old Castle Pelletized Dolomitic Lime, Hi-Yield Dolomitic Lime (use ONLY if Mg deficiency confirmed)

## Lime application mechanics

- After application, pH change takes **3–6 months** in typical clay soils to become measurable. Piedmont heavy clay may take longer.
- Do NOT recommend retesting pH sooner than 6 months after application.
- "Watering in" lime is NOT a strict requirement — lime is incorporated by rainfall and natural soil processes over months. Light irrigation after application is fine but is not the activation mechanism.
- Lime does NOT need to "activate" before fertilization can resume. Fertilization can proceed on a normal schedule after lime application. Do NOT recommend waiting 3–4 weeks after lime before fertilizing.

## Forbidden combinations — pH-correction context

**At low pH with lime program: do NOT also recommend ammonium sulfate fertilizer.** Ammonium sulfate is acidifying — it will COUNTERACT the lime and further lower pH. Use urea, calcium nitrate, or a neutral N source instead.

This also applies to MESA (Methylene Extended Sulfur Ammonium) — the slow-release coating is ammonium sulfate, which is still acidifying. Do NOT recommend MESA alongside a lime correction program for acidic soils.

## Spring pre-emergent timing — soil-temp-trigger decision tree (CRITICAL for NC Piedmont / spring)

Pre-emergent timing for crabgrass in NC Piedmont is sensitive in spring. At 68°F daytime air temperature, **soil temperature at 2-inch depth is typically 55–60°F under sunny conditions, 50–55°F under cloudy conditions**. This means the spring pre-emergent window may already be opening, closing, or closed depending on the recent weather pattern.

**DECISION TREE based on current soil temp (use ECONet for NC, your state's soil-temp tool elsewhere):**

- **Soil temp < 50°F:** Window not yet open — pre-emergent application is premature. Plan to apply when soil temps reach 50–53°F as an early-start option, or 55°F at 2" depth maintained for 3 consecutive days (the standard PSU/Purdue trigger).
- **Soil temp 50–55°F:** OPTIMAL APPLICATION WINDOW — apply prodiamine (Andersons Barricade 0.5G, generic prodiamine 65 WDG) or pendimethalin (Scotts Halts) at label rate now. Water in 0.25–0.5" within 3–5 days for prodiamine, 14 days for pendimethalin.
- **Soil temp 55–60°F:** Window is CLOSING. Apply within 5–7 days if not yet applied. Use a faster-activating product (prodiamine over pendimethalin) and water in immediately.
- **Soil temp > 60°F:** Window has PASSED for spring crabgrass. Crabgrass germination may already be underway. Do NOT frame this as the "ideal window." Pivot to:
  - Watch for crabgrass emergence (look for first true leaves at ground level)
  - Post-emergent options: quinclorac (Drive XLG, Ortho Crabgrass Killer for Lawns), or Tenacity (mesotrione) on cool-season turf
  - Plan fall pre-emergent for winter annuals (Poa annua, chickweed) at ~70°F soil temp falling, typically September–early October

**Alternate trigger if soil thermometer not available:** Forsythia bloom (the bright yellow shrub) reliably correlates with 50–55°F soil temps in the same region. Apply pre-emergent when forsythia is in full bloom.

## Pre-emergent and fall overseeding incompatibility

Tall fescue lawns in NC are commonly overseeded in fall to thicken bunch-type stands. **Pre-emergent herbicides COMPLETELY INHIBIT seed germination** — never recommend both in the same season.

- If the homeowner wants to overseed in September, skip fall pre-emergent that year (Poa annua control can be managed with post-emergent in spring if needed).
- If pre-emergent is essential (heavy Poa annua history), use **siduron (Tupersan)** — the only pre-emergent compatible with seeding. Tupersan is hard to find at homeowner level; suggest a local Lesco/JDL dealer.
- Do NOT recommend prodiamine, pendimethalin, dithiopyr, or any standard pre-emergent within 12 weeks of intended seeding.

## Phosphorus and potassium — NC-SPECIFIC rules

**North Carolina restricts broadcast phosphorus application without soil test justification.** Many NC soils have adequate or excess P from years of fertilization; over-application contributes to runoff and water quality issues.

- DO NOT recommend P-containing fertilizers (16-4-8, 10-10-10, etc.) on tall fescue without a current soil test confirming P deficiency.
- Default fertilizer recommendation without soil test P data: a **P-FREE product** — e.g., 18-0-3, 16-0-8, 25-0-5, or Milorganite 6-4-0 (Milorganite's P is organic/slow-release and generally acceptable under NC ordinances at homeowner rates).
- If soil test shows P deficiency (Mehlich P < 25 ppm, low/very-low rating): a low-P starter product like 18-24-12 at seeding is acceptable.
- K should be applied per soil test; many NC clay soils have adequate K already.

## NC Piedmont fall N program for tall fescue

Fall is the primary fertilization season for tall fescue in NC (drives root growth, prepares for winter).

- **Total annual N for tall fescue in NC: 2.5–3.5 lbs N per 1,000 sq ft** (NCSU TurfFiles)
- **Spring N: minimal — ≤0.5 lbs N/1,000 sq ft, applied only if clear deficiency is evident.** Avoid heavy spring N — it increases summer brown patch disease pressure (Rhizoctonia solani).
- Fall split applications:
  - **Mid-September: 1 lb N** (slow-release source — IBDU, polymer-coated urea, Milorganite 6-4-0 if P is acceptable; otherwise a P-free slow-release urea)
  - **Mid-October to mid-November: 1 lb N** (similar slow-release; can use late-season "winterizer" formulations here)
  - **Thanksgiving (mid-November) optional 3rd application: 0.5–1 lb N** quick-release urea — drives late root growth without top growth, builds carbohydrate reserves for spring green-up
- Skip summer N entirely — tall fescue is heat-stressed in NC summer and N application increases brown patch disease pressure.

## Tall fescue mowing height

- Standard: **3–4 inches**
- Summer stress period: **4 inches** is the upper end during high heat
- Spring/fall: **3.5 inches** is a good default (not below 3 inches)
- NEVER below 3 inches — too low reduces drought and disease tolerance

## Common framing mistakes to AVOID

- Do NOT state a specific lime rate without acknowledging the buffer-pH dependency in the same recommendation.
- Do NOT recommend 40–50 lbs/1,000 sq ft as a "conservative" rate without buffer pH — 25–30 lbs is the actual conservative blind-application rate.
- Do NOT recommend 16-4-8 (or any P-containing) fertilizer on NC tall fescue without confirming P need from soil test.
- Do NOT cite a single soil temperature trigger (e.g., "apply when soil reaches 55°F") without distinguishing whether the current temp is already at, above, or below that threshold.
- Do NOT recommend overseeding AND pre-emergent in the same fall season (except siduron).
