# Perennial Ryegrass Maintenance Program

Perennial ryegrass (Lolium perenne) is a **PERENNIAL** cool-season grass widely used in zones 5–7 as a primary turf species. It is finer-textured than tall fescue and has different management requirements.

## Botanical accuracy
- Perennial ryegrass is a PERENNIAL — persists year to year. Do NOT call it an annual.
- It can thin under sustained summer heat stress but is not an annual species.
- It is finer-textured than tall fescue — do NOT apply tall fescue heights (3–4") to perennial ryegrass.

## Mowing height
- **Normal home lawn range: 2.0–3.0 inches** (OSU/Purdue Extension residential PRG guidance)
- **Spring and heat-stress periods: 2.5–3.0 inches** — upper end is the therapeutic target heading into summer
- Reel-mown sport/golf turf: 0.5–1.5 inches (NOT applicable to home lawns)
- Do NOT recommend 1.5 inches or below as a target for residential PRG — too low for home lawn conditions, reduces heat tolerance and recovery
- Do NOT recommend below 2.0 inches for residential applications

## Nitrogen timing — FALL is primary, spring is minimal
PRG fertilization follows the cool-season pattern but with TIGHTER spring N restrictions than tall fescue or KBG.

**FALL (primary season):**
- Mid-September: 1 lb N per 1,000 sq ft (slow-release: IBDU, polymer-coated urea)
- Mid-October to mid-November: 0.75–1 lb N per 1,000 sq ft
- This builds root reserves and prepares for winter survival
- Fall N is the foundation of PRG performance the following year

**SPRING (minimal):**
- Maximum 0.25–0.5 lbs N per 1,000 sq ft
- Apply ONLY if lawn shows clear deficiency
- Use SLOW-RELEASE source: IBDU, polymer-coated urea, or Milorganite 6-4-0
- DO NOT recommend Scotts Turf Builder 32-0-10 or any other high-quick-release N product for spring PRG
- Heavy spring N promotes excessive shoot growth, depletes carbohydrate reserves before summer heat stress, and increases disease pressure (red thread, rust)

**SUMMER:**
- No N applications during peak summer heat — let PRG ride out heat stress without lush growth promotion

## Pre-emergent on PRG
- Spring crabgrass pre-emergent: apply at 50–55°F soil temp standard window
- Fall winter annual pre-emergent: 70°F soil falling
- PRG-safe options: prodiamine, pendimethalin, dithiopyr (all label-safe on established PRG)
- Spring overseeding of PRG is uncommon but if planned, defer pre-emergent or use siduron (Tupersan)

## Crabgrass + fall pre-emergent — DISTINCTION
A common error: confusing crabgrass with Poa annua in fall.
- **Crabgrass (Digitaria spp.)** is a SUMMER ANNUAL — germinates in spring, dies by first frost. Fall pre-emergent does NOT target crabgrass.
- **Poa annua, chickweed, henbit, hairy bittercress** are WINTER ANNUALS — germinate in fall, persist through winter, die in late spring/early summer.
- Fall pre-emergent on PRG targets WINTER ANNUALS (Poa annua / chickweed). DO NOT frame fall pre-emergent on PRG as "for crabgrass prevention" — crabgrass season is already over.

## Quinclorac on PRG — label safety
- Quinclorac (Drive XLR8, Bayer All-In-One Lawn Weed Killer) is the standard post-emergent for crabgrass breakthrough
- PRG tolerance is generally acceptable at labeled rates, BUT label includes safety caveats — confirm specific product label says "safe on perennial ryegrass" before recommending
- Application during cool weather (60–80°F daytime) minimizes injury risk; avoid during summer heat stress

## Diseases on PRG
- Red thread (Laetisaria fuciformis) — pink/red web-like mycelium on blades; favored by 60–75°F + nitrogen deficiency. First-line treatment: nitrogen application (light) often resolves; fungicide rarely needed.
- Gray leaf spot (Pyricularia grisea) — affects PRG aggressively in transition zones; same QoI-first treatment with rotation as on St. Augustine
- Pythium blight (P. aphanidermatum) — warm humid conditions >85°F; mefenoxam first line
- Brown patch (Rhizoctonia solani) — humid 75–85°F; QoI or DMI fungicide

## Climate suitability note
PRG performs best in zones 5–7. In Zone 8+ (lower AL/MS/GA/FL/SC coast/TX/AZ), perennial ryegrass typically functions as an annual due to summer heat — commonly used for WINTER OVERSEEDING on dormant warm-season lawns rather than as permanent turf.
