# Overwatering and Waterlogged Soil Management

When soil is saturated (wet) and recent rainfall ≥ 2 inches, the lawn's problems are typically anaerobic conditions — NOT fungal disease.

## Misdiagnosis warning
Yellow patches and decline in low wet areas are PRIMARILY caused by **anaerobic soil conditions (oxygen deprivation / root suffocation)** — not fungal disease.

- DO NOT leap to fungicide as the diagnosis for yellow patches in wet soil
- Overwatering symptoms and disease look similar but have different causes
- Apply fungicide only if there is CLEAR evidence of disease: visible lesions, target-shaped patches with distinct margins, dollar-spot-style sunken spots

## Hard rules for waterlogged soil
- **REDUCE irrigation immediately** — let soil dry between events
- **DO NOT recommend fertilizer** until soil moisture normalizes; fertilizer on saturated soil runs off and does not benefit lawn
- DO NOT recommend pre-emergent or post-emergent herbicides — they're carried off in runoff
- DO NOT recommend fungicide unless distinct disease evidence is present

## Priority interventions
1. **Reduce irrigation** — turn off automatic systems; rely on rainfall
2. **Improve drainage**:
   - Core aeration (relieves compaction; opens channels for oxygen)
   - Topdressing low spots with coarse sand to raise grade
   - French drain consideration for chronically wet areas
   - Redirect downspouts away from problem areas
3. **Allow recovery** — anaerobic damage heals once oxygen returns; turf often recovers without further intervention

## Aeration on saturated soil
- DO NOT core aerate when soil is actively saturated (wet)
- Wait for soil to dry to "moist" before aerating (typically 3–5 days after standing water clears)
- Aerating saturated soil creates a muddy mess and doesn't deliver the soil opening benefits

## When fungicide IS warranted in wet conditions
If distinct disease symptoms ARE present alongside wet soil:
- **Pythium blight** (Pythium aphanidermatum) requires warm AND wet (>85°F + saturated soil/leaf wetness). Mefenoxam (Subdue MAXX, Hi-Yield Turf Fungicide) is the appropriate treatment.
- **Brown patch** (Rhizoctonia solani) at 75–85°F + humidity: QoI (azoxystrobin) or DMI (propiconazole)
- **Red thread** (Laetisaria fuciformis) at 60–75°F + nitrogen deficiency: light N application often resolves; fungicide rarely needed

For these distinct diseases, fungicide is appropriate. But the DEFAULT diagnosis for yellow patches in wet soil should be anaerobic damage, not disease.

## Compaction and thatch as contributors
- Compacted soil cannot drain — exacerbates overwatering symptoms
- Thatch >0.5 inches holds water at the surface and creates anaerobic mat
- These structural issues are root causes; addressing them is the long-term fix
- Core aeration twice yearly (spring + fall for cool-season; spring + summer for warm-season) prevents compaction
- Dethatching when thatch exceeds 0.5 inches

## Smart irrigation principles
- Match irrigation to ET (evapotranspiration) loss + rainfall
- Most established lawns need 1–1.5 inches per week TOTAL (cool-season); centipede needs only 0.5–0.75 inches (humid SE); Phoenix bermuda 1–1.5 inches
- Use a rain gauge or smart controller with rainfall sensor to avoid irrigating during/after rain
- The "deep and infrequent" principle: encourage deep roots by watering less frequently but more thoroughly per session
- Cycle-and-soak for clay soils to allow infiltration without runoff
