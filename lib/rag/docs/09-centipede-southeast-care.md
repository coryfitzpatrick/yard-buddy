# Centipede Grass Maintenance Program (Clemson HGIC)

Centipede is a low-input warm-season grass dominant in the coastal Southeast. Its management is fundamentally different from bermuda or zoysia — over-fertilization causes "centipede decline."

## Annual nitrogen rate — STRICT CAP
- **Maximum annual N: 1 lb N per 1,000 sq ft TOTAL for the entire year** (Clemson HGIC standard for established centipede on average soils; up to 2 lbs N/1,000 sq ft/yr is acceptable ONLY when a soil test confirms sandy, low-CEC soils with documented deficiency. Default to 1 lb N/1,000 sq ft/yr unless soil-test evidence is present.)
- Maximum per application: 0.5 lbs N per 1,000 sq ft
- 1–2 split applications per year (June + August in most of Southeast)
- ANNUAL TOTAL MATH: 2 split applications of 0.5 lbs N each (each per 1,000 sq ft) = 1.0 lb N per 1,000 sq ft for the year — that IS the MAXIMUM
- UNIT CONSISTENCY RULE — CRITICAL: Express EVERY nitrogen figure on a "per 1,000 sq ft" basis. NEVER mix a "per 1,000 sq ft" rate with a "total for the entire lawn" figure in the same sentence. Wrong: "0.5 lbs N per 1,000 sq ft × 4,000 sq ft = 2.0 lbs N total, within the 1 lb cap." That reads as a contradiction even though it isn't. Correct: "Apply 0.5 lbs N per 1,000 sq ft per application; over two splits that totals 1.0 lb N per 1,000 sq ft per year, the Clemson maximum. (For a 4,000 sq ft lawn, that's 2.0 lbs of N product weight in total per application — confirm the math but always cite the rate per 1,000 sq ft.)"
- NEVER recommend "target 2 lbs of N per 1,000 sq ft" or "total of 2 lbs N per 1,000 sq ft" for centipede — this would be double the safe annual limit and cause centipede decline

## Rate expression rule — NITROGEN ONLY, never product weight
- ALWAYS express centipede fertilizer rates in NITROGEN TERMS: "apply 0.5 lbs nitrogen per 1,000 sq ft"
- NEVER state product weight: do NOT say "apply 3.3 lbs of product"
- For fungicide and herbicide products on centipede: provide spreader setting (e.g., "set Scotts rotary spreader to 3¼") — never lbs per 1,000 sq ft

## Banned products on centipede
- **Pennington UltraGreen 30-0-4** — too high N concentration; impossible to apply 0.5 lb N rate accurately
- **Pennington UltraGreen 30-0-10** — same issue
- **Lesco Stressgard** (any formulation) — too high N for centipede
- **Scotts Turf Builder** (any formulation) — too high N for centipede

## Safe centipede fertilizer products
- **Hi-Yield 15-0-15** — appropriate N concentration, phosphorus-free (preferred for established centipede)
- **Lebanon Pro 16-4-8** — acceptable
- **Milorganite 6-4-0** — slow-release, low N concentration; phosphorus may be a concern on established turf (see below)

## Phosphorus on centipede
- Excess phosphorus contributes to centipede decline AND may violate local fertilizer ordinances (FL, NC)
- DO NOT recommend P-containing products on established centipede unless soil test confirms P deficiency
- For established centipede: use phosphorus-free product like Hi-Yield 15-0-15
- Milorganite is acceptable but P-containing — confirm soil test first

## Primary diseases — gray leaf spot first, NOT dollar spot
- **Gray leaf spot (Pyricularia grisea)** is the PRIMARY summer disease threat on centipede in Southeast
  - Conditions: hot humid weather, daytime >80°F, nighttime >70°F
  - Symptoms: small gray/tan lesions with brown borders on blades
  - Treatment: same QoI-first approach as on St. Augustine (Scotts DiseaseEx / azoxystrobin)
- **Large patch / brown patch (Rhizoctonia solani)** — affects the collar region; cool wet conditions (60–75°F)
- **Dollar spot (Clarireedia jacksonii)** is NOT a primary disease concern for centipede. Do NOT lead with dollar spot recommendations on centipede.

## Centipede irrigation — significantly LESS than other warm-season grasses
Centipede is notably drought-tolerant and requires much less water than bermuda or St. Augustine.

- **Humid coastal Southeast** (Charleston SC, Savannah GA, Jacksonville FL, coastal NC — ZIP codes 29xxx, 30xxx, 31xxx, 32xxx): **0.5–0.75 inches total per week** (rainfall + irrigation)
- **Drier inland Southeast:** 0.75–1 inch per week maximum
- DO NOT apply cool-season irrigation targets (1–1.5 inch/week) to centipede — overwatering promotes disease and contributes to centipede decline
- During summer rainfall patterns common to coastal SE (0.5+ inches/week), supplemental irrigation is often NOT needed

## Centipede mowing height
- Standard: **1.5–2 inches** (Clemson HGIC)
- Maintain a single height through the growing season — do NOT scalp in spring
- Do NOT raise above 2 inches in summer (taller centipede tends to thatch)

## USDA zone reference for South Carolina coast
- **Charleston SC (ZIP 29401–29499): Zone 8b** — NOT 9a (commonly misidentified)
- Columbia SC (29201–29299): Zone 8a

## Texas / Florida note
- "Charleston, SC" is in Zone 8b — centipede goes fully dormant in winter (below 50°F)
- Coastal FL and TX have warmer winter ranges where centipede may stay partially green
