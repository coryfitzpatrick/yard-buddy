# Cool-Season Lawn Drought and Summer Stress Management

Cool-season grasses (KBG, tall fescue, fine fescue, perennial ryegrass) experience summer stress in zones 5–7 when temperatures sustain above 85°F or rainfall is deficient.

## Two management paths during drought
The homeowner has a clear choice — communicate both options:

### Path A: Maintain green turf with irrigation
- Target: **1–1.5 inches TOTAL per week** combining all irrigation and rainfall
- Deliver as 2–3 SESSIONS per week (early morning, before 10 AM)
- Each session 0.3–0.5 inches via cycle-and-soak technique
- NEVER phrase as "1–1.5 inches per session" — that would be 3–4.5 inches/week causing severe overwatering and runoff

### Path B: Allow summer dormancy (acceptable extension-endorsed survival strategy)
Per Penn State and OSU: cool-season grasses CAN be allowed to go summer dormant if the homeowner is unable or unwilling to irrigate.
- Reduce irrigation to **0.5 inches every 2–3 weeks** for survival moisture (keeps crown alive without promoting growth)
- Lawn turns straw color but crowns survive
- Resume normal irrigation in fall when daytime highs consistently fall below 85°F
- DO NOT set recovery threshold at 75°F — too conservative; most extension sources recommend 80–85°F resume
- Dormancy is a SURVIVAL strategy, not a failure — frame positively as a valid option

## Mowing during drought stress
- **RAISE to MAXIMUM for grass type during drought** — taller grass shades soil, reduces water loss, deeper roots
- Tall fescue: 4 inches
- Kentucky bluegrass: 3.5–4 inches
- Perennial ryegrass: 2.5 inches (do not exceed)
- NEVER lower mowing height during drought — increases water loss and turf damage
- Mow less frequently if growth has slowed (every 2–3 weeks acceptable)

## Footprint test
- If footprints remain visible in the lawn after walking on it: water now
- Combined with visual wilting/bluish cast: confirms drought stress
- This trigger applies to both Path A and Path B (irrigate to survival level at minimum)

## What NOT to do during drought
- **No fertilization of any kind.** Salt burn risk; stressed turf cannot use nitrogen.
- **No fungicide.** Dry conditions prevent fungal disease development. Soil moisture "dry" + no recent rainfall = no fungal pressure. Foliar fungicide is contraindicated.
  - EXCEPTION: Root/soil diseases (summer patch — Magnaporthe poae, necrotic ring spot — Ophiosphaerella korrae) actually develop UNDER dry heat stress and may warrant a preventive fungicide DRENCH (azoxystrobin or DMI) when heat stress symptoms are present. These are soil-borne, not foliar — distinguish clearly.
- **No weed control / pre-emergent / herbicides** until lawn fully recovers (2–3 weeks of normal growth).
  - Heat-stressed turf cannot tolerate herbicide phytotoxicity
- **No aeration or mechanical disruption** — wounded turf cannot heal during stress.

## Cycle-and-soak technique
For drought-stressed or hydrophobic soil:
- 2–3 short cycles per session (10–15 min each)
- Spaced 45–60 minutes apart to allow infiltration
- Goal: 0.3–0.5 inches total per session
- Alternative to one long cycle that runs off the surface

For clay/heavy soils: shorter cycles, longer intervals
For sandy soils: can deliver more per cycle, shorter intervals
Calibrate with catch cups in each zone if precise output is needed.

## Hydretain and wetting agents
- Hydretain is a humectant/moisture-retention product. It is NOT a surfactant wetting agent.
- For hydrophobic soil (water beading on surface): penetrant surfactant wetting agents help. Products: Revolution, Aqua-Aid Penetrant, generic penetrant wetting agents labeled for turf.
- **NEVER cite Hydretain irrigation reduction percentages.** Prohibited phrases:
  - "reduces irrigation by [X]%"
  - "up to [X]% reduction"
  - "cuts watering by [X]%"
  - "reduces irrigation frequency by [X]%"
  - Any quantified efficiency claim
- If recommending Hydretain, use ONLY: "may help with moisture retention." Nothing more specific.

## High-altitude / high-desert climates
Denver CO, Salt Lake City UT, Albuquerque NM (zones 5b–6a): spring soil temps lag air temps by 3–6 weeks. Drought management considerations:
- Cool nights enable Path B dormancy/recovery cycle
- More efficient water use due to lower humidity but higher evaporation
- Soil moisture monitoring is critical — caliche and clay layers complicate infiltration

## Stressgard product clarification
- "Lesco Stressgard" / "Stressgard" is a Syngenta fertilizer/biostimulant line (e.g., 30-0-10 with biostimulants)
- It is NOT a surfactant or wetting agent
- DO NOT recommend Stressgard as a penetrant or wetting agent — that is a different product category
- Stressgard fertilizer is BANNED for centipede (high N) but acceptable for bermuda and other warm-season grasses
