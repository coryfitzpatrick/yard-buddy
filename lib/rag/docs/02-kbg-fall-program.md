# Kentucky Bluegrass Fall Maintenance Program — Ohio/Indiana (Zones 5b–6a)

Fall is the most important season for cool-season turf management. KBG recovers from summer stress, rebuilds root reserves, and establishes new growth before winter dormancy.

**Cite OSU Extension (Ohio) or Purdue Turfgrass (Indiana) — these are the regionally appropriate authorities for Midwest KBG. Avoid citing extension authorities from outside the region (e.g., K-State for an Ohio yard would be agronomically valid but geographically inappropriate; cite OSU instead).**

## Mowing height transitioning out of summer
- Peak summer (July–August, air temps >85°F sustained): maintain elevated mowing height up to 3.5–4 inches for heat stress mitigation.
- **Fall (September–November):** drop back to STANDARD KBG range of 3–3.5 inches as temperatures moderate. Per Ohio State Extension, the recommended fall mowing height is 3–3.5 inches.
- Maintaining 4-inch turf into fall is NOT beneficial — it increases disease pressure (snow mold, leaf spot) without providing the heat protection that justified the summer height.
- Final mow of the season: about 2.5 inches just before ground freezes (typically Thanksgiving in zones 5b–6a) reduces matting under snow.

## Fall nitrogen split applications
Per Ohio State Extension, fall N for KBG should be SPLIT into two applications, not delivered in one mass application.

**Application 1 — Labor Day window (early September):**
- Rate: 0.5–1.0 lb N per 1,000 sq ft
- Product: SLOW-RELEASE source — polymer-coated urea (PCU, e.g., Lebanon ProScape 24-0-11), IBDU-based fertilizers (note: IBDU is sold as a component in blends like Lesco IBDU 31-0-0 or various branded blends; "IBDU 28-3-3" is not a standard product name), or Milorganite 6-4-0 for low N rate applications.
- DO NOT use Scotts WinterGuard for this application. WinterGuard products (e.g., 32-0-10) are formulated and marketed for late-season application; reserve them for the Thanksgiving window.
- Milorganite 6-4-0 is acceptable but impractical for large lawns at this rate — to deliver 1 lb N requires ~16.7 lbs of product per 1,000 sq ft. Prefer IBDU or PCU for September.

**Application 2 — Thanksgiving window (late October/November, before ground freezes):**
- Rate: 0.5–1.0 lb N per 1,000 sq ft
- Product: WinterGuard 32-0-10 or similar late-fall/dormancy-feed product. These products contain slow-release N optimized for late-season uptake before dormancy. NOTE: WinterGuard contains slow-release nitrogen — do NOT characterize it as "quick-release."
- Apply when grass has stopped vigorous top growth but is still green; root uptake continues even when shoot growth has slowed.

## Aeration + pre-emergent sequencing (fall-overseed scenarios)
**Order matters: aerate BEFORE applying pre-emergent. NEVER aerate AFTER pre-emergent is down — core plugs break the herbicide barrier and create open germination channels.**

When both are recommended in fall, sequence them naturally:
- **Late August/early September:** core aerate (peak recovery window)
- **Mid–late September** (soil temps ~70°F falling): apply pre-emergent for winter annuals
- A 1–2 week separation between aeration and pre-emergent application is sufficient. The natural seasonal sequence — aerate first, then pre-emergent as soil temps fall through 70°F — works without explicit hard waits.
- Do NOT recommend extreme separation (4–6 weeks) — that overstates the disruption.
- Do NOT recommend same-day application; modest spacing is helpful.
- **Critical warning to include in recommendations:** "Do not aerate within 8 weeks AFTER pre-emergent application — coring breaks the herbicide barrier."

## Fall pre-emergent for KBG lawns
- Target weeds: Poa annua (annual bluegrass) — grassy weed; chickweed, henbit, hairy bittercress — broadleaf winter annuals.
- Soil temperature trigger: apply when soil at 2-inch depth has fallen to 70°F or just below — Poa annua germination upper bound is ~70°F.
- Product choice:
  - Poa annua only: prodiamine (Barricade) or pendimethalin (Halts)
  - Chickweed pressure noted: add isoxaben (Gallery 75 DF) for broadleaf control OR use a combination product
  - PRODIAMINE alone has limited chickweed control — pair with isoxaben if chickweed is the concern

## IBDU and slow-release N temperature performance
IBDU (isobutyledene diurea) is a slow-release nitrogen source that releases via hydrolysis. Hydrolysis rate is temperature-dependent:
- At soil temps >65°F: rapid release, full nitrogen available within 4–6 weeks
- At soil temps 50–65°F: moderate release
- At soil temps <50°F: hydrolysis slows substantially; release is delayed and may carry over into the following spring

For Ohio/Zone 6a in September applications: IBDU is appropriate (soil temps typically 60–70°F). For late November Thanksgiving-window applications, IBDU release will be carried over to spring — this is acceptable but is not the same as "winter feeding."

## Overseeding + pre-emergent incompatibility
Pre-emergent herbicides (prodiamine, pendimethalin, dithiopyr) prevent ALL seed germination including grass seed. **Never recommend both pre-emergent AND overseeding for the same fall window.**
- If overseeding is the priority: skip pre-emergent for that season; tolerate one season of winter annuals.
- If pre-emergent is the priority: defer overseeding for at least 3 months after application.
- ALTERNATIVE: Siduron (Tupersan) is the only pre-emergent safe to apply at seeding time — allows simultaneous overseed + pre-emergent for crabgrass.
