# Grass Type Identification Guide (Cool-Season vs Warm-Season)

When grass type is unknown, identification is the FIRST priority — every species has different mowing heights, fertilization needs, disease pressures, and herbicide tolerances.

## Initial classification — cool-season vs warm-season
**Where you live tells you what you likely have:**
- Zone 5–7 (Midwest, Northeast): cool-season dominant (KBG, tall fescue, perennial ryegrass, fine fescue)
- Zone 8 transition: mix of both; warm-season more common
- Zone 9–10 (Southeast, Southwest): warm-season dominant (bermuda, zoysia, St. Augustine, centipede, buffalo)

**Seasonal behavior:**
- Cool-season: actively grows in spring and fall; struggles in summer heat (browns or thins); green through fall and into winter in mild zones
- Warm-season: greens up in late spring; thrives in summer; goes DORMANT (brown/straw) when temperatures drop below 50°F sustained

## Key visual identifiers

### Spreading mechanism
- **Stolons only** (above-ground runners): St. Augustine, centipede
- **Rhizomes only** (underground stems): Kentucky bluegrass, tall fescue (limited rhizomes)
- **Both stolons + rhizomes**: bermuda, zoysia
- **Bunch-type** (no spreading runners): perennial ryegrass, fine fescue

### Blade width
- **Very fine** (< 2mm): fine fescue, Zoysia matrella (Zeon, Cavalier), bermuda hybrid
- **Fine** (2–3mm): Zoysia japonica (Meyer, Z-52), perennial ryegrass, bermuda common
- **Medium** (3–5mm): Kentucky bluegrass, centipede
- **Wide** (>5mm): St. Augustine (very wide), tall fescue (medium-wide)

### Blade tip & shape
- **Boat-shaped** (pointed-cupped): Kentucky bluegrass — distinctive identifier
- **Pointed**: tall fescue, perennial ryegrass
- **Rounded/blunt**: St. Augustine, centipede

### Other distinguishing features
- **Tall fescue:** coarse texture, prominent VEINS (not midrib), rolled vernation, bunch-type growth. DO NOT identify tall fescue by "prominent midrib" — that's St. Augustine.
- **Perennial ryegrass:** prominent midrib visible from below, glossy underside of blade, folded vernation
- **St. Augustine:** prominent midrib, very wide blade (5+ mm), rounded tip, stoloniferous spreading runners
- **Kentucky bluegrass:** boat-shaped pointed tip, medium-width blade, rhizomatous (underground spreading)
- **Bermuda:** very fine blade with small ligule hairs (fringe), grayish-green color, dense low-growing

### Vernation (how new leaves emerge from sheath)
- **Folded**: perennial ryegrass, KBG, zoysia
- **Rolled**: tall fescue, bermuda, St. Augustine
- (Less useful for homeowner identification; mention if relevant)

## Quick identification flow
1. **Is the lawn green right now in winter?** (below 50°F sustained)
   - YES: cool-season grass (KBG, fescue, PRG)
   - NO (brown/dormant): warm-season grass (bermuda, zoysia, SA, centipede, buffalo)
2. **Where is the lawn?**
   - Northern US (Zone 5–7): cool-season
   - Southern US (Zone 8–10): warm-season
3. **What do the blades look like?** Apply blade width + tip + spreading mechanism above
4. **What's the recent care history?** Type of sod laid (if known), prior seeding, common grass for the region

## What to do when grass type is unknown
- LEAD with grass type identification guidance in your recommendations
- Suggest: take close-up photos and submit to a local Cooperative Extension office for identification
- Self-identification guide: provide the key visual differences above so homeowner can assess
- Until identified, give GENERIC guidance with explicit uncertainty framing

## Generic guidance when type is unknown
- **Mowing height**: 2–3.5 inches as a generic safe range. Caveat that specific range depends on species.
- **Fertilization**: do NOT recommend specific N rates or NPK formulas. Provide only general timing guidance ("warm-season grasses fertilize during active summer growth; cool-season grasses fertilize fall + light spring").
- **Disease + pest**: defer species-specific advice until identified. General monitoring guidance acceptable.
- **Pre-emergent**: frame as general guidance without species-specific timing — soil temperature triggers still apply but optimal product depends on species

## Cornell Cooperative Extension resources (Northeast)
- For ZIP codes in NY state (10xxx–14xxx): nearest Cornell Cooperative Extension county office can help with identification
- For NYC specifically (ZIP 10xxx): **CCE New York City — cceny.org** (the correct NYC CCE site). Cornell's CALS extension portal is **harvest.cals.cornell.edu**. AVOID citing "ccenyc.org" (incorrect domain) or "ccetompkins.org" (that is the Tompkins County office in Ithaca, not NYC).
- For soil testing in NY area: Cornell Soil Health Laboratory (Cornell University, Ithaca) or UMass Soil Testing Lab (acceptable cross-state for NYC homeowners). DO NOT recommend Whitetail Institute — that's a food plot brand, not a turf soil test lab.
- Submitting clear photos of a 4×4 inch lawn sample + blade close-up usually allows identification

## Bermuda is unlikely in NYC (Zone 7a)
- DO NOT cite bermuda grass as a comparison example for mowing height when discussing NYC lawns — bermuda is extremely unlikely at Zone 7a; could mislead the homeowner about realistic grass type possibilities
- Likely candidates for NYC area: Kentucky bluegrass, tall fescue, fine fescue (cool-season)
