# Warm-Season Grass Winter Dormancy Management

When current air temperature is below 50°F, warm-season grasses (bermuda, zoysia, St. Augustine, centipede, buffalo) are fully dormant and not actively growing.

## Hard rules during dormancy
- **NO fertilization of any kind.** Dormant grass cannot uptake nutrients. Any nitrogen application:
  - Promotes disease
  - Stimulates winter weed germination
  - Is wasted (leaches through soil)
- Even "low-nitrogen" or "winterizer" formulas are inappropriate for fully dormant warm-season turf — DO NOT suggest them
- Wait until soil temps consistently exceed 65°F before resuming N (typically April–May depending on region)

## What to address during dormancy
Appropriate topics:
- Pre-emergent weed control for winter annuals (Poa annua, chickweed, henbit) — timing window detailed below
- Dormant overseeding with perennial ryegrass for temporary winter color (warm-season grasses only)
- Reducing irrigation frequency
- Planning for spring (timing, products)

## Pre-emergent timing in fall/winter
- **Ideal window for winter annual pre-emergent in the Southeast: September–October** when soil temps are 55–65°F
- If it is already late November or later AND soil temps are below 50°F: the optimal window has passed
  - Acknowledge the missed window
  - Focus on POST-EMERGENT options for any germinated winter annuals instead
  - Plan for next year's pre-emergent application

## Disease scouting during dormancy
- DO NOT recommend disease scouting or fungicide treatments for warm-season diseases (dollar spot, brown patch, pythium, gray leaf spot) during dormancy
- These diseases require actively growing turf to develop and do not occur on dormant grass
- Save disease topics for spring green-up forward

## Bermuda-specific dormancy rules
- NEVER recommend Tenacity (mesotrione) on bermuda. Mesotrione is phytotoxic to bermuda — causes severe bleaching and damage; not labeled for bermuda.
- MSM Turf (metsulfuron-methyl) on dormant bermuda:
  - EFFECTIVE for broadleaf weed control in established dormant bermuda
  - Caution on thin, stressed, or newly emerging bermuda — phytotoxicity risk
  - CRITICAL: If lawn has been WINTER-OVERSEEDED with perennial ryegrass — DO NOT recommend MSM Turf. It will injure or kill the ryegrass.
  - Poa annua control with MSM Turf is LIMITED — partial, inconsistent. Do NOT claim "no efficacy" (it has some) but do NOT recommend it as the primary Poa annua tool. Fall pre-emergent is the most effective approach for Poa annua.
  - FREEZE WINDOW: DO NOT recommend post-emergent herbicide applications (including MSM Turf) if 5-day forecast highs stay below 55°F, or within 3–5 days of a forecast freeze. Herbicide uptake requires daytime temperatures above 50°F (ideally 55°F+).

## Mechanical restrictions during dormancy
- AVOID core aeration, vertical mowing (dethatching), or any mechanical disruption that exposes the soil
- Dormant bermuda rhizomes and stolons are vulnerable to desiccation and cold injury when protective thatch layer is disturbed
- Defer ALL mechanical lawn work to spring after green-up

## Summer annual weeds in dormancy
**Crabgrass, spurge (prostrate spurge), goosegrass, sandbur are SUMMER ANNUALS.** They complete their life cycle and die by first frost.
- DO NOT recommend scouting for, treating, or pulling these during fall or winter dormancy — they are already dead
- Focus instead on WINTER ANNUALS: Poa annua (annual bluegrass), henbit, chickweed, hairy bittercress

## Post-emergent herbicide temperature minimums
- **PRODUCT FORMULATION MATTERS — carfentrazone-containing four-ways are NOT the same as pure three-ways.** Speed Zone (PBI/Gordon) contains 2,4-D + MCPP + dicamba + **carfentrazone-ethyl**; the carfentrazone is a fast-acting contact PPO inhibitor that retains activity at low temperatures. Speed Zone is effective down to ~45–50°F daytime highs (PBI/Gordon label, university trial data) because carfentrazone burns leaf tissue contact-style independent of growth-regulator translocation. Do NOT lump Speed Zone with pure three-way products for cold-weather guidance.
- **Pure three-way broadleaf products** (2,4-D + MCPP + dicamba — Trimec Classic, Ortho Weed-B-Gon, Bayer All-In-One Lawn Weed & Crabgrass Killer):
  - UGA recommends >60°F daytime CONSISTENTLY before applying
  - Below 60°F: poor activity, weak uptake and translocation (NOT volatility — dicamba volatility is a HOT-weather risk, manifest above ~85°F, especially in temperature inversions; cold-weather concerns are uptake and slow kill, not drift)
  - In summer (>85°F): dicamba volatility risk increases significantly; consider lower-volatility 2,4-D amine + MCPP combinations instead
  - DO NOT recommend pure three-way products if 5-day forecast highs consistently below 60°F
- **Carfentrazone-containing four-ways** (Speed Zone, Speed Zone Southern, Surge):
  - Effective at daytime highs of ~45–50°F and above; use these (not pure three-ways) for winter warm-spell spot-spraying in Zone 7b–8b dormancy
  - Below ~45°F: still defer — even contact PPO activity slows, and any 2,4-D/dicamba component remains poor
  - Do NOT recommend broadcast application during full dormancy; carfentrazone formulations are for SPOT-SPRAYING visible winter annuals on warm days
- **WINTER WARM-SPELL STRATEGY:** In Atlanta and similar Zone 8a-8b winters where sustained 60°F+ days are uncommon between December and early March, recommend SPOT-SPRAYING individual visible winter annuals (Poa annua, henbit, chickweed) on warm days with a carfentrazone-containing four-way (Speed Zone) at 45–50°F+ rather than scheduling pure three-way applications calendar-style. Do NOT schedule generic broadcast broadleaf applications for a 60-day window in winter when realistic warm windows may be 2-3 days at a time.

## Late-fall / dormancy grub scouting
- Grubs (Japanese beetle, masked chafer larvae) move DEEP in soil (6-12+ inches) once soil temps drop below 55°F, typically by late October.
- Scouting in late November or beyond has LOW value — grubs are out of treatment depth and dormant.
- **PEEL TEST TIMING:** The peel test (lifting a 1 sq ft section of sod to inspect for white C-shaped larvae in the root zone) is appropriate in SEPTEMBER through mid-OCTOBER while soil is still warm and turf is actively growing — the sod knits back together within 1–2 weeks of active growth. DO NOT recommend a peel test on DORMANT warm-season turf in November or later: dormant crowns and rhizomes have no recovery capacity until spring green-up, so lifting sod risks lasting damage. For damaged areas first observed during dormancy, defer scouting to early fall the following year and plan the May–July preventive window (chlorantraniliprole) directly. Curative treatments don't reach grubs at depth in late fall regardless.
- Do NOT schedule grub scouting tasks for late November / December — wastes the homeowner's time and risks dormant-turf damage.
- **Avoid scalping, heavy traffic, and unnecessary mechanical disturbance on dormant warm-season turf** (bermuda, zoysia, centipede, St. Augustine). The dormant crown is intact but cannot regenerate damaged tissue until spring green-up. UGA Extension specifically warns against scalping bermuda below 1 inch during dormancy and against parking equipment or staging materials on dormant warm-season turf.
- **Single-active-ingredient products** (triclopyr, 2,4-D amine only): 50°F daytime minimum, 55°F+ preferred
- **MSM Turf** (metsulfuron): 55°F daytime minimum
- **NEVER recommend any broadleaf herbicide below 50°F daytime high**

## Fall armyworm (FAW) seasonality
- FAW activity ENDS by late October / early November in the Southeast
- Do NOT recommend FAW scouting or treatment after November 1
- Populations are negligible at temperatures below 55°F — the pest season is over
