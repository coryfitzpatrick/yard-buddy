# Managing High-pH Calcareous Soils (Denver Front Range, Zone 5b–6a)

Front Range Colorado soils are predominantly CALCAREOUS — they contain free calcium carbonate (CaCO3) that continuously buffers against acidification. This makes pH correction fundamentally different from non-calcareous soils elsewhere.

## Realistic pH correction expectations
- Achieving "ideal" turfgrass pH (6.0–6.5) via elemental sulfur is GENERALLY NOT achievable long-term in native Front Range soils. Free calcium carbonate continuously neutralizes sulfuric acid produced by sulfur oxidation.
- Realistic goal at pH 7.8 in Denver: reduce by 0.3–0.5 units over 1–2 seasons of consistent sulfur applications, improving iron availability. A multi-year sustained program (3–5 years) is required for any meaningful long-term reduction toward 7.0–7.4.
- DO NOT frame Denver pH correction as easily achievable. Set expectations: sulfur improves micronutrient availability over time; permanent correction to 6.5 is not realistic without continuous acidification inputs.

## Elemental sulfur application
- Product: GRANULAR ELEMENTAL SULFUR (agricultural/lawn grade — Espoma Garden Sulfur, generic "lawn sulfur" or "soil sulfur"). NOT powdered sulfur fungicide products (Bonide Sulfur Plant Fungicide, Hi-Yield Wettable Dusting Sulfur) — these are fine-particle fungicide-grade not designed for soil pH correction.
- Rate per application: 1–2 lbs per 1,000 sq ft (CSU standard for established turf). Start at the LOWER end (1 lb) in warm/dry/sunny conditions to avoid phytotoxicity.
- **ANNUAL CUMULATIVE MAXIMUM: 5 lbs per 1,000 sq ft per YEAR** (CSU Extension established turf cap). This means at 1–2 lbs per application, you can apply 3–5 split applications per growing season — but the seasonal total must not exceed 5 lbs.
- Spacing: allow 4–6 weeks between applications.
- Always water in immediately after application.
- Never exceed 5 lbs in any single year on established turf.

## Soil temperature requirement (CRITICAL for Denver)
Elemental sulfur acidification depends on MICROBIAL OXIDATION — Thiobacillus bacteria convert elemental S to sulfuric acid. This process requires soil temperatures consistently ABOVE 55°F.

In Denver/Front Range (Zone 5b–6a):
- November through March: soil temps typically below 55°F. Sulfur applied during this window sits INERT — does not acidify.
- Best application windows: late April through October when soil temps are reliably 55–60°F+.
- Always mention the soil temperature requirement when recommending sulfur in early spring to Denver homeowners.

## Combined sulfur + EDDHA iron approach
Per CSU, when high pH is causing iron chlorosis symptoms (interveinal yellowing in new growth), apply BOTH:
1. **Sulfur** — long-term pH correction (slow, multi-year). Apply per schedule above.
2. **EDDHA chelated iron** — immediate iron availability for symptom relief.

Do NOT skip sulfur because results are slow — it remains the only correct soil amendment for high pH.

## Ammonium sulfate (21-0-0) framing
Ammonium sulfate is appropriate for high-pH soils because of its ACIDIFYING effect — the ammonium form of N acidifies soil as it converts to nitrate, and the sulfate ion contributes additional acidification.
- **It is a QUICK-RELEASE, water-soluble fertilizer.** Do NOT describe it as slow-release.
- Apply 0.5–0.75 lbs N per 1,000 sq ft per application (lower rate than slow-release products).
- More frequent applications than slow-release products.
- Its value in a high-pH program is its acidifying effect, not its release kinetics.

## Soil pH retesting timeline
- Recommend retesting 3–6 months after starting a sulfur program to track progress.
- Heavy clay/calcareous soils: meaningful nutrient availability change may take 6+ months.
- Do NOT retest sooner than 3 months — pH change in calcareous soils is slow and short-interval retesting will not show meaningful change.

## KBG specifically at pH 7.8 in Denver
- KBG ideal range: 6.0–7.0
- Realistic long-term goal in Denver calcareous soil: 7.0–7.4 over 3–5 years of consistent sulfur applications
- Combine pH program with EDDHA iron for immediate green-up while pH program works long-term
- Other micronutrients (manganese, zinc) also become unavailable at pH 7.8+ but iron chlorosis is the dominant visible deficiency
