# St. Augustine Grass Summer Maintenance (Texas A&M AgriLife)

## Mowing height
- **Standard full-sun mowing height: 2.5–3.5 inches** (Texas A&M AgriLife Extension residential St. Augustine — standard cultivars like Floratam, Raleigh, Palmetto)
- **Shaded locations: 3.5–4 inches** — taller mowing compensates for reduced photosynthesis under tree canopy.
- **Absolute minimum: 2.5 inches** for standard varieties; 2 inches for semi-dwarf cultivars (Floratam dwarf, Captiva). NEVER recommend below 2.5 inches without confirming cultivar.
- Many Houston/southern lawns have partial shade — the shade-height distinction (3.5-4") is a critical recommendation for those scenarios.
- Mowing frequency: every 5–7 days during summer; never remove more than 1/3 of blade height per mow.

## Chinch bug scouting and threshold
The southern chinch bug (Blissus insularis) is the primary summer insect pest of St. Augustine.

**Scouting method — SOAP FLUSH (drench), the Texas A&M AgriLife standard:**
1. Mix 1.5 oz liquid dish soap with 1 gallon of water
2. Pour evenly over a 4 sq ft area at the margin of damaged turf
3. Watch for 5 minutes — chinch bugs irritated by the soap will surface
4. Count adults and nymphs that emerge

Alternative: coffee-can flotation (cut both ends out of a coffee can, push 1 inch into turf, fill with water, count floating insects). The soap flush is generally preferred — easier and covers more area per test.

**Action thresholds** (Texas A&M AgriLife Extension — METHOD MATTERS, do not conflate):
- **Visual inspection / coffee-can flotation** (full population estimate per area): **20–25 chinch bugs per square foot** of damaged turf indicates treatment threshold.
- **Soap flush method** (catches a fraction of the population — irritated bugs that surface): **2–3 chinch bugs per square foot** from soap flush observations is the treatment threshold per UF/IFAS and Texas A&M. Do NOT apply the 20-25 number to soap flush counts — that would be unreasonable.

**When recommending treatment thresholds, always specify which method the count refers to.** Example: "If your soap flush brings up 8 or more chinch bugs from a 4 sq ft area (≥2 per sq ft), treatment is warranted."

**PYRETHROID RESISTANCE WARNING:** Southern chinch bug populations in the Gulf Coast (Houston, FL, coastal LA, AL) have well-documented resistance to pyrethroid insecticides (bifenthrin, lambda-cyhalothrin, permethrin). If pyrethroid treatment fails or chinch bugs return within 4 weeks, ROTATE to a different mode of action: clothianidin (Bayer Complete Insect Killer for Soil and Turf), trichlorfon (Dylox), or carbaryl (Sevin). Mention resistance management when recommending bifenthrin/Talstar in Houston or other coastal areas.

**Scouting sample area:** Texas A&M AgriLife recommends a coffee-can flotation cylinder (cut both ends out, push 1 inch into turf, fill with water, count floating insects) OR the soap flush method (1.5 oz dish soap + 1 gallon water poured over 4 sq ft area, count bugs surfacing in 5 minutes). Use ONE method consistently; do not mix counts.

Treatment when above threshold: bifenthrin (Talstar P, Ortho Bug-B-Gon) or imidacloprid (Bayer Complete Insect Killer for Soil and Turf). Apply per label; water in lightly to move active ingredient into the thatch where chinch bugs feed.

## Gray leaf spot (Pyricularia grisea) — PRIMARY summer disease
Gray leaf spot is the dominant summer disease threat on St. Augustine — NOT brown patch. Brown patch is favored by nighttime temps below 70°F (a fall/spring disease on warm-season grasses).

**Conditions favoring gray leaf spot:**
- Daytime temps >80°F and nighttime temps >70°F
- High humidity, leaf wetness >12 hours per day, recent rainfall
- Excessive nitrogen (lush succulent growth is highly susceptible)

**Lesions:** small (1-4 mm) olive-green to tan/brown spots with a distinctive WATER-SOAKED appearance and YELLOW (chlorotic) halo. Lesions can elongate into oval/elliptical shapes especially on perennial ryegrass overseeded turf, but the diagnostic features on St. Augustine are the small spot size, water-soaked center, and yellow halo. The yellow border distinguishes gray leaf spot from brown patch (which has dark border ring with green center).

**During active infection: DO NOT apply nitrogen.** Nitrogen stimulates lush growth that the pathogen exploits. Defer all N applications until disease pressure recedes (lesions stop spreading, grass actively recovering).
- The trigger to resume N is DISEASE RESOLUTION, not nighttime air temperature thresholds.
- Do NOT hold N indefinitely based on temperature alone.

**Fungicide treatment — QoI (FRAC Group 11) is first-line:**
Homeowner-accessible QoI products:
- **Scotts DiseaseEx Lawn Fungicide** (azoxystrobin) — most accessible at retail
- **BioAdvanced Disease Control for Lawns** (azoxystrobin)

DMI fungicides (myclobutanil/Spectracide Immunox, propiconazole/Banner Maxx) are NOT preferred — less reliable control of Pyricularia grisea than QoI fungicides. Do NOT lead with myclobutanil or propiconazole.

Professional formulations (Heritage, Armada 50 WDG) exist but are less accessible to homeowners — prefer Scotts DiseaseEx for homeowner recommendations.

**MANDATORY RESISTANCE MANAGEMENT (must include in every gray leaf spot fungicide recommendation):**
Pyricularia grisea has DOCUMENTED resistance to QoI/strobilurin fungicides in many regions. Always include rotation guidance:
- Apply no more than 2 consecutive Scotts DiseaseEx (azoxystrobin/QoI) applications
- Then rotate to a different mode of action: **tebuconazole** (BioAdvanced Fungus Control for Lawns) is the most homeowner-accessible DMI alternative
- Fluopyram (Indemnify) and boscalid are professional SDHI options — not retail-available

## Herbicide rules for St. Augustine
- **NEVER recommend 2,4-D on St. Augustine** — causes severe phytotoxicity, not labeled for SA
- **Sulfentrazone** (Dismiss, Dismiss South) is generally SAFE at labeled rates — avoid application when air temps consistently exceed 90°F to reduce transient discoloration risk
- **Atrazine** is commonly used and safe on SA for broadleaf and post-emergent annual grass control. It has SOME pre-emergent activity but is primarily a POST-emergent product on residential St. Augustine — for pre-emergent Poa annua control, prefer pendimethalin (Scotts Halts) or isoxaben (Gallery 75 DF). Critical timing/safety rules:
  - DO NOT apply if rain forecast within 24–48 hours
  - DO NOT apply if soil is wet/moist (runoff risk)
  - Follow label re-application intervals
  - **TEXAS LABEL — WATER BODIES:** Atrazine label specifically restricts application near water bodies, drainage ditches, storm drains, and wells. This is critical for Houston's flat, flood-prone geography. Maintain at least a 50-foot buffer from any water body or drainage feature. Atrazine is a leading contaminant of urban runoff in coastal TX waterways.

## Pre-emergent timing for SA in fall (Houston, Zone 9b)
- Target weeds: Poa annua (annual bluegrass), winter broadleaf weeds
- Soil temperature trigger: apply when soil temp at 2-inch depth approaches **70°F falling**
- For Houston/Zone 9b: this typically corresponds to **late September through early October** — NOT mid or late October. By mid-October Poa annua may already be germinating in Zone 9b coastal areas.
- Preferred products on St. Augustine: pendimethalin (Scotts Halts) or isoxaben (Gallery 75 DF for broadleaf winter annuals). Atrazine has SOME pre-emergent activity but is primarily used post-emergent on St. Augustine.
- ALWAYS include soil temperature qualifier — never recommend pre-emergent on calendar alone in Zone 9; verify with soil thermometer before applying.
