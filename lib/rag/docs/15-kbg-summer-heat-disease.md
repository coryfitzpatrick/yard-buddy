# Kentucky Bluegrass Summer Heat and Disease Management

KBG is vulnerable to heat stress and root/crown diseases in summer (July–August in zones 5–7 when daytime temps consistently exceed 85°F).

## Summer mowing height
- Raise to **3.5–4 inches** during peak summer heat stress (consistently >85–90°F)
- Taller grass shades soil, reduces water loss, supports deeper roots
- Return to STANDARD 3–3.5 inches as fall temperatures moderate (September onward)
- DO NOT maintain 4 inches into fall — provides no benefit, increases disease pressure

## Hot weather irrigation
- Target 1–1.5 inches TOTAL per week (rainfall + irrigation)
- 2–3 sessions per week, early morning (before 10 AM)
- Each session 0.3–0.5 inches via cycle-and-soak
- Deep infrequent encourages root growth; shallow frequent promotes shallow roots and disease

## Summer patch (Magnaporthe poae) — primary KBG summer disease
- **Causal pathogen: Magnaporthe poae** — DO NOT call it Magnaporthe nivalis (that is pink snow mold, a completely different cool-weather disease)
- Symptoms: rings or "frogeye" patterns of dead/yellowing turf 1–5 ft diameter
- Soil temperature trigger: 65–80°F at root zone — typical mid-summer
- Favored by drought stress, compacted soil, heavy thatch
- Preventive fungicide: DMI (propiconazole/Banner Maxx) or QoI (azoxystrobin/Scotts DiseaseEx) as SOIL DRENCH (not foliar) — must reach roots
- Apply when soil temps reach 65°F (preventive); curative is far less effective

## Necrotic ring spot (Ophiosphaerella korrae)
- Distinct pathogen from summer patch with similar symptoms
- Same temperature triggers, same drench application method
- Both root/crown diseases require DRENCH application, not foliar — fungicide must reach root zone
- Same fungicide options as summer patch

## Fungicide note for KBG diseases — DEFAULT: NO FUNGICIDE IN DRY HEAT

**Default behavior in dry heat conditions (humidity <65%, no recent rain): DO NOT recommend any fungicide application.** Discuss disease awareness as monitoring guidance only — "watch for ring/frogeye patterns if conditions stay hot and stress accumulates."

The ONLY exception that permits a fungicide recommendation under dry conditions:
1. The homeowner's profile explicitly describes visible ring or frogeye dead patches consistent with summer patch / necrotic ring spot, AND
2. The recommendation explicitly names "summer patch (Magnaporthe poae)" or "necrotic ring spot (Ophiosphaerella korrae)" in the same sentence as the fungicide, AND
3. The recommendation explicitly specifies "soil drench" application method (not foliar spray), AND
4. The recommendation specifies that irrigation must be applied with the drench to carry the product to the root zone.

If ANY of these conditions are unmet, omit the fungicide recommendation entirely and frame as future monitoring instead.

**Do NOT recommend "preventive fungicide for summer disease pressure" generically — that violates the humidity rule and is not appropriate when no symptoms are visible.**

## Product accuracy — Scotts DiseaseEx
- Scotts DiseaseEx Lawn Fungicide contains **AZOXYSTROBIN** (FRAC Group 11 / QoI strobilurin)
- DO NOT call it thiophanate-methyl (FRAC Group 1 / MBC benzimidazole) — that is the active in Scotts' older Disease Control product
- Azoxystrobin and thiophanate-methyl are different classes with different modes of action

## Cool weather KBG diseases (for context)
- **Pink snow mold (Microdochium nivale, formerly Fusarium nivale)** — winter/early spring under snow cover. Distinct from summer patch.
- **Gray snow mold (Typhula spp.)** — similar conditions
- **Red thread (Laetisaria fuciformis)** — spring and fall, nitrogen-deficient conditions; usually responds to light N application

## Botanical accuracy
- KBG spreads primarily by RHIZOMES (underground horizontal stems that produce new tillers)
- DO NOT say KBG spreads by stolons — stolons are above-ground horizontal runners; KBG does NOT produce stolons
- Bermuda and zoysiagrass have BOTH rhizomes AND stolons
- St. Augustine spreads by above-ground stolons only

## Heat-stress hard rules
When current temperature exceeds 85°F sustained:
- DO NOT recommend high-nitrogen fertilizer (heat-stress turf cannot handle quick-release N)
- DO NOT recommend specific high-N product codes (28-0, 32-0, 34-0, 30-0)
- If fertilization mentioned: "defer to fall when temperatures drop below 75°F"
- DO NOT recommend overseeding or seeding — cool-season seed cannot germinate or survive
  - Overseeding should be deferred to fall when SOIL TEMPERATURES drop to 60–65°F (verify with soil thermometer)
- Focus on heat stress management: raise mowing height, deep infrequent irrigation, avoid foot traffic

## Aeration timing for KBG
- FALL aeration (late August through October) is strongly preferred — KBG is actively recovering with weeks of growing season ahead
- Spring aeration is risky: wounding occurs just before peak summer heat stress
- In high-altitude/high-desert (Denver, SLC, Albuquerque): fall aeration especially preferred (spring soil temps lag air temps)
- NEVER recommend spring aeration for KBG unless homeowner explicitly requested it
